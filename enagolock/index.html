Protecting Academic Data, One Manuscript at a Time

Role: Product Manager

Company: Crimson Interactive

Brands Involved: Enago, Publication Support, Ulatus (PS)

Impact: lowered the order form drop-off rate by 16.8%

Strategy

Background

In 2023, a senior editor at Enago highlighted a recurring problem: high-value manuscripts sometimes included sensitive personal data like patient info, grant IDs, or unpublished results.

As remote work grew, authors became hesitant to share documents involving grants or sensitive data. Without in-person safeguards, universities worried about editorial mishandling, threatening the traditional editing model.

I collaborated with editors, clients, and tech teams to assess the issue, spoke with users, mapped workflows, and found that insecure data handling was eroding author trust in traditional editing.

Context

A casual exchange of documents feels harmless. But multiply it across hundreds of submissions, and you get:

Sensitive data floating across unencrypted personal inboxes

No access logs or audit trails

Risk of accidental forwarding or leaks

For any institution managing dozens of such papers monthly, this is a ticking time bomb.

I analyzed submission volumes and workflows to identify leak points. Using data and patterns, I demonstrated to leadership that this was a product issue, not just an operational hiccup.

The Challenge

Authors grew hesitant to share documents involving grants, IP, or sensitive data. Without in-person safeguards, universities feared editorial mishandling, weakening the traditional model.

The challenge was balancing editors’ need for simplicity and speed with universities’ demand for enterprise-grade security. My role was to design a solution that met both without slowing the process.

Solution: Patch to Platform

At the time, Enago’s tech team had a small internal tool, nothing fancy, just a basic encrypt-decrypt utility to obscure author names and emails for internal transfers. It worked, but it was clearly duct tape over a growing leak.

Here’s what we did:

  • We Created a risk map of the editing workflow with the tech team.
  • We laid out key requirements like access control, time limits, and audit logs.
  • Suggesting the .enago file extension as a visible marker so users could see their files were protected.
“

We went from encrypting text strings to protecting intellectual property at the institutional level.

How it Works

  • Authors upload files via the Inquiry form.
  • Enago encrypts the document into a .enago file
  • The file is viewable only through secure preview.
  • Editors can only access files with time-bound keys
  • Admins (authors) receive full access logs and traceability.

I mapped the full user journey and worked with engineering to ensure the system was both secure and user-friendly. Security couldn’t feel like extra work, it had to be seamlessly integrated into the existing workflow.
